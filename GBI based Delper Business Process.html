The Perpetual Loop.

A hybrid Global Basic Income model in which automated production, human wages, and a third "attention layer" — earnings from watching ads and completing surveys — feed a single circulating fund. Workers and non-workers alike consume what the system produces; that spending becomes next year's dividend and wage pool.

The Model

Three income layers, one circulating fund

Each layer is justified differently — cooperative membership, attention, or labor — so the system doesn't require everyone to hold a job, but still rewards work and keeps a funding link to real economic activity rather than printing money from nothing. Note the ownership structure: Delper owns the underlying infrastructure (factories, robotics, AI compute, data centers) and leases capacity to the global cooperative. The cooperative — not Delper — collects production and ad revenue, pays Delper rent for infrastructure use, and distributes what's left as Layer 1 and Layer 2 income to its 6 billion members. This is a landlord/tenant relationship, not shared equity — a distinction that matters for who bears the risk of a bad year and who captures the upside of a good one.

Layer 1 / Floor

Cooperative Dividend

$12T / year · ~$2,000 per person

Unconditional payout from the global cooperative's net surplus — production and ad revenue, minus the rent paid to Delper for use of its factories, robotics, and AI infrastructure. Funded by membership in the cooperative, not by owning the machines outright — Delper owns them; the cooperative rents them.

Layer 2 / Supplement

Attention Income

~$0.3–0.6T / year · realistic ceiling, see reality check below

Micro-payments for watching ads and completing surveys, funded directly by advertiser and market-research spend rather than the trust. Useful as a top-up and an on-ramp for people without formal banking access — not a substitute for the other two layers.

Layer 3 / Ceiling

Chosen-Labor Wages

~$29T / year · market-priced, ~3–4B working-age participants

Ordinary wages across healthcare, education, science, care work, automation maintenance, and any sector machines can't easily replace. This is the layer that rewards skill and effort.

Reality check — why Layer 2 can't be a third of the loop

Global advertising spend across every channel (TV, digital, print, out-of-home) totals roughly $1 trillion a year worldwide, and market-research budgets add a small fraction more. That entire industry — every ad shown to every person on earth — is smaller than one-tenth of the $41T target.

~$1TTotal global ad spend / yr, all media
~$700BDigital share of that spend
~$0.30–0.60TRealistic ceiling if a large share were redirected to a user fund
<2%Of the $41T target this layer could plausibly cover

Redirecting even 40–50% of all global ad spend into a user-payout pool — a radical restructuring of the entire advertising industry, since publishers, agencies, and production still need to be paid — would generate on the order of $0.3–0.6T/year. Divided across 6 billion people that's roughly $50–100/year each: a genuinely useful supplement (data costs, phone credit, small discretionary spend) but nowhere near a living floor on its own. It works best as bootstrapping income in Tier 3/4 countries before dividend and wage infrastructure exists, and as a way to fund the digital ID/banking rollout itself.

Rent & governance terms — what 10% + veto actually does to the loop

Two new terms were set: Delper charges 10% per annum on infrastructure value, and Delper holds veto over the cooperative. Both change the math and the power balance, not just the paperwork.

The rent math. Producing ~$41T/year of goods needs a large base of factories, robotics, and AI compute behind it. Using a typical capital-to-output ratio for capital-intensive manufacturing (~2–2.5x), that infrastructure is worth roughly $85–100T. At 10%/year, rent alone is:

~$9–10TRent owed to Delper / year
~$50–51TGross output the cooperative must generate to still net $41T for people
~9–10 yrsTime for Delper to recover the full infra value in rent alone
~3–4xTotal rent collected vs. infra value over a 30-year horizon

10%/year is a high rate for infrastructure specifically — real-world infrastructure funds typically target 6–10% total return (rent plus eventual resale or depreciation credit) over the life of an asset, not a flat 10% of value paid out every single year on top of that. A flat 10%/year in perpetuity means Delper collects the equivalent of the entire infrastructure's value roughly every decade, indefinitely. Either global production has to grow ~20–25% larger than the $41T target just to cover rent, or the $12T dividend layer absorbs most of that cost and shrinks toward $2–3T — closer to $350–500/person/year instead of $2,000.

The veto. Structurally, veto sitting with Delper rather than the cooperative means the 10% rate — and any future rate change — isn't something members can renegotiate through governance; it's set unilaterally by the party being paid. That's the exact concentration risk flagged earlier, now made concrete: whoever sets the rent controls the size of the dividend, and here that's Delper, not the cooperative. This is a workable business arrangement (a landlord charging a lease rate and holding approval rights is normal in commercial real estate and infrastructure finance), but it functions as an extraction point rather than a mutual-benefit cooperative unless the rate is renegotiable or the cooperative holds some counterbalancing right — a rent cap, a sunset clause, or co-veto on rate changes.

This is a modeling exercise, not a proposal with institutional backing. The $41T global wage/dividend target, the tier assignments, and the attention-income ceiling are illustrative figures meant to show how the pieces would need to fit together — real implementation would require multilateral tax treaties, new international institutions, and national-level infrastructure that mostly don't exist yet.

One structural point now baked into the numbers above: with Delper setting a flat 10%/year rent and holding veto over the cooperative, the rate isn't negotiable by the membership — it's set by the party collecting it. That's not disqualifying on its own (landlord/tenant infrastructure deals work this way all the time), but it does mean the $2,000/person dividend figure used earlier in this model is optimistic; the rent math above suggests the real net figure lands closer to $350–500/person unless output scales up to cover the rent or the rate is renegotiated.

Sources for orders of magnitude: global ad spend figures are commonly-cited industry estimates (WARC / GroupM / Zenith range, ~$1T total, ~$700B digital, as of recent years) rather than a single verified figure — treat them as approximate. Country tier assignments are heuristic groupings for this model only and should not be read as an official readiness ranking of any country.
